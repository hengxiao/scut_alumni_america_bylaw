# Status Quo: Independent Status and Organizational Autonomy

## What the Current By-Laws Say

**Article I §2:** States that the organization is incorporated in California as a Non-Profit Mutual Benefit Corporation. No mention of independence from any foreign institution or government.

**Article III §1(d)–(e):** The purposes of the organization include:
- "(d) To enhance the connection between the members and South China University of Technology; and"
- "(e) To help and support the development of South China University of Technology, and increase its worldwide reputation."

These provisions create an expressed purpose of supporting a foreign university but contain no language defining the boundaries of that relationship or affirming the organization's independence from SCUT or the Chinese government.

**Article III §3:** States that the organization "shall not participate in any political activities and other activities that may jeopardize its tax-exempt status." This is the only provision addressing the organization's political posture — but it is framed solely around tax-exempt status, not organizational independence or the U.S. political and regulatory environment.

No other provision addresses the organization's relationship with foreign governments, foreign institutions, foreign embassies or consulates, or the basis on which the organization's governance decisions are made.

## Gaps and Observations

### 1. No Explicit Independence Declaration

The current By-Laws contain no statement that the organization is independent of SCUT, the People's Republic of China, the Chinese Communist Party, or any foreign government body. In the current U.S. political and regulatory environment, this silence is a material governance gap. Any organization with a name-based connection to a Chinese institution that cannot point to explicit independence language in its governing documents faces heightened scrutiny.

### 2. Purposes Language Could Be Read as Institutional Subordination

Article III §1(e) commits the organization to "help and support the development of South China University of Technology." Read narrowly, this expresses alumni pride and affinity. Read broadly — particularly by a regulator applying FARA or the Foreign Missions Act — it could be interpreted as the organization operating as an extension of SCUT's institutional interests. The current By-Laws contain no limiting language clarifying that the organization's support for SCUT is voluntary, member-driven, and subject to independent organizational judgment rather than institutional direction.

### 3. No Prohibition on Foreign Government Oversight

The current By-Laws contain no provision prohibiting the organization from accepting guidance, direction, oversight, or approval from any foreign government, embassy, consulate, or state-affiliated institution. This is the provision that was missing from CSSA chapters that drew congressional scrutiny in 2026.

### 4. No Leadership Independence Provision

The current By-Laws do not address whether any external party — including SCUT, a Chinese government body, or a Chinese consulate — has any formal or informal role in the selection, approval, or removal of the organization's officers or directors. There is no explicit statement that the organization's leadership is chosen solely by its members under its own procedures.

### 5. No Member Privacy Protection from Foreign Disclosure

The current By-Laws contain no provision protecting member information (names, contact details, professional affiliations) from disclosure to any foreign government entity. If the organization were found to be sharing member data with SCUT, the Chinese Embassy, or a Chinese consulate, this would expose both the organization and individual officers to legal liability.

### 6. No Financial Independence Policy

The current By-Laws (Article VII) govern how funds are deposited and managed but do not address whether the organization may accept funding from foreign government entities, state-affiliated Chinese institutions, or Chinese state-owned enterprises. Accepting such funding without FARA registration could constitute a violation of federal law.

### 7. No Response Protocol for Foreign Influence Attempts

The current By-Laws contain no procedure for how the organization responds if a foreign government body, institution, or official attempts to assert authority over its activities, communications, or leadership. There is no mechanism for the Board to document and decline such attempts, nor any provision authorizing officers to report such attempts to appropriate authorities.

## Summary

The current By-Laws say nothing about organizational independence. In 2002, when these By-Laws were adopted, this was a common oversight for community organizations. In 2026, with Congress actively considering designating Chinese-affiliated organizations as foreign missions and with FARA enforcement receiving renewed attention, the absence of explicit independence provisions is a material risk. A revised By-Laws must:

- Explicitly declare the organization's independence from all foreign governments and institutions.
- Clarify the nature of the relationship with SCUT (alumni affinity, not institutional dependency).
- Prohibit foreign government oversight, direction, or approval of any organizational function.
- Affirm that leadership selection is solely a function of the membership under the organization's own procedures.
- Protect member information from unauthorized disclosure to any foreign entity.
- Establish financial independence guardrails.
- Provide a response protocol if foreign influence is attempted.
