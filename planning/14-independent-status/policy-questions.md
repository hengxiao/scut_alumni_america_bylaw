# Policy Questions: Independent Status and Organizational Autonomy

These are the questions the organization must answer before drafting the independence provisions of the revised By-Laws. Each question reflects a real policy choice with legal and reputational consequences in the current U.S. regulatory environment.

---

## 1. Should the By-Laws include an explicit organizational independence declaration?

The single most important question in this topic area. The absence of such a declaration is the defining characteristic of organizations that have drawn congressional scrutiny.

- Should the By-Laws include a standalone article or section that explicitly states the organization is independent from SCUT, the Chinese government, the CCP, and any foreign government body?
- Should the independence declaration be placed in the Purposes article (Article III), as a standalone article, or in both?
- Should the declaration identify specific entities from which independence is affirmed (SCUT, PRC, CCP, Chinese embassy/consulates) or use broader language covering all foreign governments and institutions?
- Should the independence declaration be designated as a "core provision" that requires a higher amendment threshold — similar to how USTCAF treats certain governance articles — to make it harder to quietly remove in the future?

---

## 2. How should the By-Laws characterize the organization's relationship with SCUT?

The current By-Laws commit the organization to "enhance the connection between the members and South China University of Technology" and "help and support the development of South China University of Technology." These purposes are legitimate expressions of alumni affinity but must be carefully framed.

- Should the current language in Article III §1(d)–(e) be retained as-is, revised, or replaced?
- If revised, should the new language explicitly state that the connection to SCUT is one of *alumni affinity* rather than institutional representation or agency?
- Should the By-Laws explicitly state that the organization does not act as a representative or agent of SCUT?
- Should the By-Laws state that any cooperation with SCUT (e.g., joint events, information exchanges, scholarship coordination) requires Board approval and is subject to the organization's own independent judgment — not SCUT's direction?
- Should there be a limit on the types of cooperation with SCUT that the organization may undertake — for example, cultural and educational events are permitted, but activities that could constitute lobbying, information gathering, or fundraising on SCUT's behalf are not?

---

## 3. Should the By-Laws prohibit acceptance of operational guidance from foreign government entities?

This is the provision that was missing from CSSA chapters that accepted guidance from the Chinese consulate-general.

- Should the By-Laws include an explicit prohibition on accepting operational guidance, instructions, or direction from any foreign government, embassy, consulate, or state-affiliated institution?
- Should the prohibition extend to informal communications and relationships, not just formal written directives?
- Should the prohibition apply only to foreign governments generally, or should it specifically name the Chinese government and related entities?
- If an officer or director receives unsolicited guidance from a foreign government representative, should the By-Laws require them to decline, document, and report the contact to the Board?

---

## 4. Should the By-Laws explicitly state that leadership selection is solely a function of the membership?

Congressional scrutiny of the CSSA included specific findings that the Chinese Embassy asserted approval authority over some CSSA chapter leadership candidates.

- Should the By-Laws explicitly state that no foreign government, institution, embassy, consulate, or official has any role in nominating, approving, confirming, or removing any officer or director?
- Should this provision be placed in the Elections article (Article V or equivalent), the independence article, or both?
- Should the Board be required to affirmatively document, at each election cycle, that the election process was conducted without any foreign influence or external approval requirement?
- Should the By-Laws include a provision that any purported approval or veto of leadership candidates by a foreign entity is void and without effect?

---

## 5. Should the By-Laws prohibit loyalty pledge requirements for membership?

Congressional scrutiny of the CSSA identified chapters whose membership applications required applicants to affirm loyalty to Chinese government policy objectives.

- Should the By-Laws explicitly prohibit any membership requirement that asks members to pledge loyalty, deference, or allegiance to any foreign government, political party, or foreign official?
- Should the By-Laws explicitly state that membership eligibility is based solely on the criteria set forth in the By-Laws (SCUT alumni/student status), with no foreign government-imposed condition?
- Should the Board be required to review membership application materials periodically to confirm that no loyalty pledge or foreign-government-imposed condition has been introduced?

---

## 6. What member privacy protections should the By-Laws establish?

Surveillance of Chinese students abroad — including reporting of member information to Chinese government entities — was a central concern in the congressional findings about the CSSA.

- Should the By-Laws explicitly prohibit officers and directors from disclosing member names, contact information, professional affiliations, or organizational activities to any foreign government, embassy, consulate, or institution?
- Should the prohibition apply equally to disclosure in response to formal requests from foreign government entities and to informal information sharing with foreign government officials?
- Should the By-Laws specify that member information may be disclosed to foreign entities only if required by applicable U.S. law — and that even in such cases, the Board must be notified and legal counsel consulted?
- Should the organization adopt a separate member privacy policy (referenced in the By-Laws) that addresses data storage, access controls, and disclosure rules in more detail?

---

## 7. What financial independence guardrails should the By-Laws establish?

An organization that accepts funding from a foreign government source may be required to register under FARA. The By-Laws should address this risk proactively.

- Should the By-Laws prohibit the organization from accepting funds from any foreign government, state-owned enterprise, or state-controlled institution?
- Alternatively, should the prohibition be limited to funds whose acceptance would require FARA registration or that condition the funds on the organization acting at the direction of a foreign entity?
- Should the Treasurer be required to disclose the source of all significant contributions to the Board — including whether any contribution comes from a source with foreign government affiliation?
- Should the Board be required to obtain legal counsel review before accepting any contribution exceeding a defined threshold (e.g., $1,000) from a foreign source?
- Should the By-Laws prohibit the organization from providing financial benefits (sponsorships, marketing support, in-kind services) to foreign government entities or state-affiliated institutions?

---

## 8. Should the By-Laws establish a protocol for responding to foreign influence attempts?

The By-Laws can make the independence provisions operational — not merely declarative — by specifying what officers and directors must do if a foreign entity attempts to assert influence.

- Should the By-Laws require any officer or director who receives a communication that purports to direct or influence the organization's governance to promptly notify the Board?
- Should the Board be required to document such communications and the organization's response?
- Should the By-Laws require the Board to consult legal counsel to assess whether any notification or reporting obligation under U.S. law (e.g., FARA, the Foreign Missions Act) is triggered?
- Should the Board be required to notify members if the organization receives a foreign influence attempt — and if so, in what timeframe and in what level of detail?
- Should the By-Laws authorize the Board to take corrective action — including removing an officer or director who cooperates with an unauthorized foreign influence attempt — without a full membership vote?

---

## 9. How should the By-Laws balance openness to SCUT's legitimate academic programs with independence safeguards?

Some activities that genuinely serve members' interests may involve SCUT or SCUT-related entities — for example, alumni career fairs that include SCUT faculty, scholarship programs funded by SCUT alumni, or cultural events that SCUT's overseas alumni office may wish to co-sponsor.

- Should the By-Laws distinguish between cooperation with SCUT's academic or cultural programs (generally permitted) and cooperation with Chinese government agencies, the Chinese embassy, or Chinese consulates (prohibited)?
- Should any cooperation with SCUT or SCUT-affiliated entities above a defined level (e.g., co-branding, financial contribution, joint programming) require Board approval and be documented to demonstrate that the organization retained independent decision-making authority?
- Should the By-Laws include a general principle — for example, that the organization will not enter into any arrangement with any entity, foreign or domestic, that conditions the arrangement on the organization ceding control over its governance, activities, or communications?
