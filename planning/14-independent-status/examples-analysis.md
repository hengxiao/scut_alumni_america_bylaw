# Examples Analysis: Independent Status and Organizational Autonomy

**Planning Topic:** 14 — Independent Status and Organizational Autonomy
**Question:** How should SCUT Alumni America's By-Laws establish and protect its independent status as a U.S.-based organization?

---

## Background: The 2026 Congressional Scrutiny of Chinese-Affiliated Organizations

In March 2026, three U.S. House committee chairs (Select Committee on China, House Foreign Affairs Committee, House Education and Workforce Committee) formally asked Secretary of State Rubio to designate the Chinese Students and Scholars Association (CSSA) as a foreign mission of China. The letter identified specific provisions in CSSA governing documents that triggered this designation request:

| Risk Factor | Description |
|---|---|
| Express consulate oversight | SWCSSA constitution stated the organization "accepts the guidance of the People's Republic of China Consulate-General in Los Angeles" |
| Leadership approval by embassy | Chinese Embassy asserted approval authority over CSSA leadership candidates at some chapters |
| Loyalty pledge membership requirements | Some chapters required membership applicants to affirm loyalty to Chinese government policy objectives |
| Member surveillance | CSSAs described as monitoring and reporting on Chinese students abroad |

**Key Lesson:** The legal and reputational risk does not require actual misconduct. Organizations with name-based connections to Chinese institutions are at risk if their governing documents do not proactively and explicitly address independence. A bylaw that is silent on independence will be read in the most adverse light by a regulator looking for a reason to act.

## How the 10 Reference Organizations Address Independence

### Overview

Independence provisions are rare in the bylaws of the 10 reference organizations because most were drafted before the current regulatory environment. However, three patterns emerge:

| Organization | Independence Language | Approach |
|---|---|---|
| PUAASC | Partial | Non-partisan clause; no explicit foreign government independence statement |
| PKUAA-NE | Partial | 501(c)(3) non-partisan language; no explicit foreign government independence statement |
| USTCAF | Strong | Core article prohibiting political activities; non-partisan clause as immutable provision |
| FDAAGLA | None | No independence provisions identified |
| XTUAANA | None | No independence provisions identified |
| CTUAAA | None | No independence provisions identified |
| SJTU-SV | Partial | Non-partisan clause; 501(c)(3) public benefit framing |
| HCSSA | Partial | University student org — Harvard policies govern, creating institutional dependency |
| TAAGNY | Partial | 501(c)(3) non-partisan language |
| OCA | Strong | Explicit non-partisan and independence provisions as a U.S. advocacy organization |

**Key Finding:** None of the 10 reference organizations includes an explicit provision prohibiting foreign government oversight or affirming independence from the home country institution. This gap reflects the pre-2026 regulatory environment. SCUT Alumni America should be among the first wave of Chinese-affiliated alumni associations to explicitly address this.

## Part 1: Relevant Legal Frameworks

### Foreign Agents Registration Act (FARA) — 22 U.S.C. § 611 et seq.

FARA requires persons who act as agents of a foreign principal — including foreign governments and foreign political parties — to register with the U.S. Department of Justice and disclose their activities.

**An organization is at risk of FARA registration if it:**
1. Acts at the direction or control of a foreign government or state-affiliated institution.
2. Engages in political activities in the U.S. on behalf of a foreign principal.
3. Acts as a public relations or informational representative of a foreign principal.
4. Solicits funds or other things of value within the U.S. for a foreign principal.

**Bylaw Provisions That Reduce FARA Risk:**
- An explicit declaration that the organization does not act at the direction of any foreign government or institution.
- A prohibition on accepting operational guidance from foreign embassies, consulates, or governmental bodies.
- An explicit statement that the organization's relationship with SCUT is voluntary alumni affinity, not institutional agency.

**Note:** Bylaw provisions alone do not eliminate FARA risk if actual conduct contradicts them. However, explicit independence provisions are a necessary (though not sufficient) component of maintaining independent status.

### Foreign Missions Act — 22 U.S.C. § 4301 et seq.

The Foreign Missions Act allows the Secretary of State to designate an organization as a foreign mission if it is substantially owned or effectively controlled by a foreign government. If designated:
- The organization must register with the State Department.
- It must obtain State Department approval for certain public events and meetings with government officials.
- Its property and personnel may be subject to State Department oversight.

**Bylaw Provisions That Reduce Foreign Missions Act Risk:**
- Explicit prohibition on any foreign government body having ownership, voting rights, or control over the organization.
- Prohibition on any foreign government approval being required for the organization's leadership, activities, or communications.
- Documentation of member-driven governance as the sole basis for organizational decisions.

### Internal Revenue Code — 501(c)(7) and Political Activity

The organization's existing IRS 501(c)(7) status (addressed in Topic 13) already prohibits certain political activities. However, IRS rules address campaign intervention in U.S. elections — not foreign government influence. An organization can comply fully with IRS political activity rules while still operating under foreign government direction. Topic 14 addresses a distinct, non-overlapping risk.

## Part 2: Model Independence Provisions from Other U.S. Contexts

No Chinese university alumni association in the reference set has an explicit independence provision. However, model language can be drawn from:

### Model A: Non-Governmental Organization Independence Declaration (International NGO Standard)

Used by U.S.-based organizations that receive foreign funding or have foreign institutional relationships:

> "This organization is an independent, U.S.-based nonprofit corporation. It is not an agent, representative, or instrument of any foreign government, political party, or governmental institution. All decisions regarding the organization's governance, activities, finances, and communications are made solely by the organization's members and elected leadership under U.S. law and the organization's own governing documents. No foreign government, institution, embassy, or consulate has any authority to direct, guide, approve, or veto any action of this organization."

### Model B: OCA (Organization of Chinese Americans) Approach — Explicit U.S. Civic Identity

OCA's bylaws frame the organization's identity in terms of U.S. civic participation and explicitly distinguish advocacy for Asian American communities in the U.S. from any foreign government interest:

> "OCA advocates for the well-being of Asian Pacific Americans in the United States. OCA does not act as a representative of, or at the direction of, any foreign government or institution. OCA's positions reflect the views of its U.S.-based members and leadership."

**Adaptation for SCUT Alumni America:**
> "The Association is organized to serve the interests of its members in the United States. The Association is not organized to represent, act on behalf of, or at the direction of, South China University of Technology, the People's Republic of China, or any other foreign government or institution. The Association's relationship with South China University of Technology is one of voluntary alumni affinity and does not constitute an agency or representative relationship."

### Model C: Institutional Independence Through Governance Exclusivity

Used by foundations and associations that have name-based connections to foreign institutions but wish to maintain independent governance:

> "Membership in the Board of Directors is open only to members of this Association. No person may serve on the Board of Directors by appointment of, or subject to confirmation by, any foreign government, institution, or official. All officers and directors are elected or appointed solely under the procedures set forth in these By-Laws."

## Part 3: Specific Independence Provisions for SCUT Alumni America

### 1. Organizational Independence Declaration

The most important provision: a clear, affirmative statement that the organization is independent. This is the provision that CSSA chapters lacked.

**Recommended Language:**
> "The Association is an independent organization. It is not subject to the direction, guidance, oversight, or approval of South China University of Technology, the People's Republic of China, the Chinese Communist Party, any Chinese government ministry, or any Chinese embassy, consulate, or official. All governance decisions are made solely by the Association's members and elected leadership under U.S. law and these By-Laws."

### 2. Relationship With SCUT — Alumni Affinity, Not Institutional Agency

The current By-Laws' reference to supporting SCUT's development must be preserved (it is a legitimate expression of alumni pride) but bounded.

**Recommended Language:**
> "The Association's connection to South China University of Technology is one of alumni affinity. The Association may engage in activities that celebrate the university's heritage, maintain professional connections among its graduates, and promote the cultural and educational interests of its members in relation to their SCUT experience. The Association does not represent SCUT, act as SCUT's agent, or conduct activities at SCUT's direction. Any cooperation with SCUT is voluntary, member-approved, and governed solely by the Association's own decision-making procedures."

### 3. Prohibition on Foreign Government Oversight

**Recommended Language:**
> "No foreign government, political party, embassy, consulate, or official of any foreign country shall have any authority to direct, guide, control, or approve the Association's activities, communications, finances, or governance decisions. The Association shall not accept operating instructions from any foreign government entity. The Association shall not include in any membership application, governing document, or organizational commitment any pledge of loyalty or deference to any foreign government or political party."

### 4. Leadership Independence

**Recommended Language:**
> "All officers and directors of the Association shall be elected or appointed solely under the procedures set forth in these By-Laws. No foreign government, institution, embassy, consulate, or official shall have any formal or informal role in nominating, approving, confirming, or removing any officer or director of the Association."

### 5. Member Privacy Protection

**Recommended Language:**
> "The Association shall not disclose member names, contact information, professional affiliations, or any other member information to any foreign government, institution, embassy, consulate, or official, except as required by applicable U.S. law."

### 6. Financial Independence

**Recommended Language:**
> "The Association shall not accept funds, grants, in-kind contributions, or any thing of value from any foreign government, political party, embassy, consulate, or state-owned or state-controlled enterprise if such acceptance would require the Association to act at the direction of, or in the interests of, the contributing foreign entity. Before accepting any significant contribution from a foreign source, the Board shall consult legal counsel to assess FARA registration obligations and other applicable legal requirements."

### 7. Response Protocol for Foreign Influence Attempts

**Recommended Language:**
> "If any officer or director receives a communication from a foreign government, embassy, consulate, or foreign institutional representative that purports to direct, instruct, or influence the Association's governance, activities, or communications, the officer or director shall promptly report such communication to the Board. The Board shall document the communication, decline to act upon any unauthorized direction, and shall consult legal counsel to assess whether any reporting obligation under federal law applies."

## Recommendations for SCUT Alumni America

### Critical (Legal and Reputational Risk Without These)

1. **Add an explicit organizational independence declaration.** This is the single most important provision in light of the 2026 congressional scrutiny of Chinese-affiliated organizations. Every Chinese university alumni association that cannot point to such language is at elevated risk.

2. **Scope and preserve the SCUT relationship.** The current purposes language is not a problem if it is accompanied by clear limiting language. Reframe Article III §1(d)–(e) as expressing alumni affinity, explicitly disclaim agency, and clarify that any cooperation with SCUT is voluntary and member-approved.

3. **Add a prohibition on foreign government oversight.** Explicitly prohibit any foreign government, embassy, consulate, or official from directing, approving, or conditioning any aspect of the organization's governance. This provision directly addresses the language pattern that triggered congressional scrutiny of the CSSA.

4. **Add leadership independence language.** Explicitly state that officers and directors are chosen solely under the organization's own procedures, with no foreign government role.

5. **Prohibit loyalty pledges.** Explicitly prohibit any membership requirement, organizational commitment, or governing provision that requires members to pledge loyalty or deference to any foreign government or political party.

### High Priority

6. **Add member privacy protections.** Prohibit disclosure of member information to foreign government entities. This protects members who hold security clearances, government positions, or sensitive professional roles.

7. **Establish financial independence guardrails.** Require Board review and legal counsel assessment before accepting significant contributions from foreign government sources.

### Medium Priority

8. **Establish a response protocol.** Give officers and directors clear guidance on what to do if a foreign government entity attempts to assert authority over the organization. This makes the independence provisions operational rather than merely declarative.
